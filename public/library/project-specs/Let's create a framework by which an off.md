When a politician in the United States takes office, the oath is usually brief and tied to the Constitution, but it carries with it a wide set of implicit promises to the people. It would be fair to say that by the time they assume office, they have already committed themselves, in principle, to the following general duties:

### 1. Fidelity to the Constitution and Rule of Law

* To uphold and defend the Constitution of the United States and, where relevant, the state constitution.
* To follow the laws duly enacted and respect the legal framework of government.
* To resist any attempt to subvert constitutional order.

### 2. Representation of the People

* To act as a voice for their constituents in legislative, executive, or judicial processes.
* To consider the interests, rights, and needs of the people who elected them.
* To maintain a degree of accessibility and accountability to the public.

### 3. Stewardship of Public Resources

* To use taxpayer money responsibly, avoiding waste, fraud, or misuse.
* To oversee public funds and programs with integrity, ensuring they serve the intended public purposes.
* To treat their role as a trust, not a personal enrichment scheme.

### 4. Protection of Rights and Freedoms

* To safeguard the civil liberties guaranteed by the Constitution.
* To avoid actions that would unjustly deprive citizens of their rights.
* To respect the equal protection of the law for all groups.

### 5. Good Faith and Ethical Conduct

* To act honestly in the exercise of their duties.
* To avoid corruption, conflicts of interest, or abuse of office.
* To maintain loyalty to the public interest above private gain.

### 6. Commitment to Peaceful Governance

* To follow democratic processes and norms, accepting election results and transitions of power.
* To resolve disputes through lawful and orderly means.
* To support the continuity and stability of government institutions.

### 7. Service and Duty

* To carry out the responsibilities of the office diligently and competently.
* To prioritize the public good in decision-making.
* To recognize their role as a servant of the people rather than as a ruler over them.

These are not always spelled out in the oath itself, but they are the widely understood commitments that accompany holding office in a constitutional democracy.
